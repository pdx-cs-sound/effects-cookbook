AGC vs. limiter — what a static curve can and can't show

Left: steady-state transfer curves — AGC and a limiter are the same line. Right: the same two effects over time, where they behave nothing alike. The thing that separates them (release time) is a time-domain property, invisible on a static curve.

The point: on the left, "AGC or limiter" is one curve — a steady-state plot cannot distinguish them. On the right, the slow AGC loop rides the whole envelope toward the target; but the gain it accumulated while boosting quiet material carries the fast transient past full scale, into the shaded clipping region — the standard reason an AGC is followed by a limiter. The limiter, by contrast, is one-sided: below the ceiling it passes the input through unchanged — drawn faint, under the dashed gray — and never boosts quiet material. The bold red is where it is actually reducing gain (drawn idealized: no attack or release). The two dashed reference lines belong to different effects: the target is where the AGC steers the average; the ceiling is the line the limiter lets nothing cross. They are set apart on purpose — aim for a comfortable level, guard a margin above it — which is the usual arrangement when the two run in series.
