    Compression¶

    Dynamic range compression turns down the loud parts of a signal, shrinking the gap between loud and quiet. The track sits more evenly and can be raised in overall level.

    Chapter 6 — companding. See also Limiting, Expanding, the slower, target-seeking AGC, and the clock-driven Tremolo of Chapter 5.

    Intuition¶

    A compressor behaves like a sound engineer with one hand on a volume fader. The fader comes down the moment the singer gets too loud and comes back up when the singer drops back. The compressor automates the hand. The threshold sets how loud is too loud, and the ratio sets how hard the fader comes down.

    Typical uses: evening out an erratic performance, taming peaks so the overall level can be raised, gluing a mix together, adding punch or sustain.

    Key parameters¶

    Parameter What it controls
    Threshold The level above which compression begins (dBFS).
    Ratio How much the overshoot is reduced. At 4:1, 4 dB over the threshold comes out as 1 dB.
    Attack How fast the gain comes down once the threshold is crossed (ms).
    Release How fast the gain recovers once the signal drops back (ms).
    Knee How abruptly the ratio engages around the threshold (hard vs. soft).
    Makeup gain Fixed boost after compression to restore overall level (dB).

    How it works¶

    1. Detect the level of the incoming signal (peak or RMS).
    2. Compare with the threshold. Below it, do nothing. Above it, compute the gain reduction from the ratio.
    3. Smooth the gain change with the attack and release time constants.
    4. Apply the smoothed gain to the signal, then add makeup gain.

    Pseudocode¶

    COMPRESS(x, threshold, ratio, attack, release, makeup)
    gain ← 0
    for each sample s in x
        level ← DETECT(s)                          ▷ peak or RMS, in dBFS
        if level > threshold
            target ← (threshold − level) · (1 − 1/ratio)
        else
            target ← 0
        gain ← SMOOTH(gain, target, attack, release)
        emit s · LINEAR(gain + makeup)

    DETECT reports a level in dBFS, SMOOTH is the one-pole follower of Chapter 5, and LINEAR converts a dB value to a linear gain. The pseudocode on the other pages of this chapter uses the same subroutines.

    Reference implementation¶

    Building a compressor involves more decisions than the pseudocode above shows: a detector, a topology, a knee shape, a place to smooth, lookahead, and makeup. The map below starts from no design. Context on the five projects is in the References appendix.

    Two facts the map understates. Not every path composes, because a feedback topology cannot look ahead at output that does not exist yet. And the map's resolution is itself a design choice, since the options listed are the coarsest set that still separates the five implementations.

    A configurable implementation whose keyword arguments are the rows of the map lives in the repository at code/compressor.py. The five reference implementations are included as presets, and compress(x, sr, **PRESETS["audacity"]) runs their path. The engine is exercised by code/test_compressor.py, and the time-domain figures in this chapter are generated by running it (code/make_figures.py).

    Pitfalls

    • Pumping and breathing: a release that is too fast makes the level surge audibly between hits.
    • dB vs. linear. Gain math lives in dB, and sample math lives in linear. Convert deliberately.
    • Lookahead: a feed-forward detector reacts after a transient begins. Limiters add lookahead to catch it; see Limiting.

    Related effects¶

    • Automatic Gain Control: the slower, target-seeking member of the family.
    • Limiting: compression with an infinite ratio and a fast attack.
    • Expanding, and the gate: the inverse; they turn down the quiet parts.
